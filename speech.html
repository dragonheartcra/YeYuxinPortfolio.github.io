Persuasive Speech

Speech by June Tan, Executive Director, Singapore Children's Society

National Parents' Forum held on 26 January 2025 at Marina Bay Sands Convention Centre

Introduction

Ladies and gentlemen, distinguished guests, and concerned parents, thank you for being here today. We are gathered here not just as professionals, educators, and policymakers but as protectors of our children's future.

Today, I rise to address an issue that refuses to take a back seat. Our children are in danger-not from the streets but from their screens. The same devices meant to connect us have also become windows to the dark world of cyberbullying, inappropriate content, and sexual predators.

Singapore has always been at the forefront of protecting its youth, but the current minimum age for access to social media-13 years-is just not enough. Research proves that children below the age of 16 years are not emotionally mature enough to use social media safely and are prone to manipulation, mental health problems, and exploitation.

Singapore Children's Society is raising a siren of alarm for urgent action: We must raise the age limit of the social media use policy to 16 years and enforce more strict regulation to safeguard our children from their digital harm.

Central Argument 1: Mental Health Crisis Among Young Social Media Users

Research by the Singapore Digital Safety Institute shows the stunning truth that cyberbullying cases have tripled in the last five years, while over 70% of the children aged 10-15 years have faced online harassment or harmful content.

Allow me to share a story with you. Amanda-13-year-old young girl-was harassed more than once on social media. Anonymous bullies made fun of her appearance while sending her hate messages, and even spread false rumors about her. Anxiety, depression, and self-isolation: that story led to such outcomes. Her parents didn't know until many months later how much damage had been done through her changed behaviors.

Unfortunately, Amanda is not alone. She is just one in the multitudes of children trapped in such experiences here in Singapore. Researchers found that those who use social media over three hours per day have triple the rate-or even twice of developing severe mental health problems for kids: low self-esteem, suicidal ideation among teens, etc., and there would be increased psychological vulnerability due specifically because their lives become too predictable or lack significant challenge present during key human developmental stage.

We have to ask ourselves: why are we putting our children through these dangers at this tender age of their lives? Raising the age limit to 16 is not about restricting freedom; it is an opportunity for children to develop emotional resilience before being released into the online world.

Central Argument 2: The Global Trend Toward Increased Regulation

We are not fighting this fight alone; all over the world, countries wake up to this reality-one harmful for young users on social media.

Australia has already passed a 16+ social media law, and in just one year, cases of cyberbullying dropped by 30%. The United Kingdom and Canada are considering similar policies. Singapore cannot afford to lag behind.

The reasons are many, but let us be honest: some may say that parental supervision is enough. Parents cannot monitor their children 24/7, and algorithms on these platforms make sure that children get to see harmful content, even when children are simply scrolling. From unrealistic beauty standards to encouraging self-harm, social media manipulates young minds in ways we're just learning about.

If we have strict legal age limits for smoking, drinking, and driving, why on earth are we giving 13-year-olds free rein on platforms that can cause lasting psychological harm?

Increasing the age limit is not a recommendation; it is an imperative.

Conclusion: A Call to Action

Parents, policymakers, educators-the time to act is now.

We cannot afford waiting until more children fall victim to online predators of cyberbullying or turn into mental hazards. Raising the minimum social media age to 16 basically is not at all about lost freedoms; rather, it covers the assurance of our budding children growing up responsibly in a safer digital timescape.

Here is what we have to do together:

  • Implement strict age verification to ensure no underage users can access any social media.
  • Push for stronger content moderation laws to take away harmful material.
  • Educate parents and teachers in the use of digital safety so that they are able to instruct children well.
